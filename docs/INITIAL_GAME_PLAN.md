# Multiplayer Dungeon Initial Game Plan

## Working Premise

This game is a 2-player, campaign-length, retro dungeon crawler with asymmetric roles, puzzle trials, combat-heavy exploration, and a 16-bit SNES-inspired presentation. The core fantasy is not just "two heroes in a dungeon"; it is two linked adventurers whose worlds, timelines, powers, and information do not fully match.

The strongest design direction is a dungeon where each player can affect the other's reality. One player might act in a past, spirit, hidden, mechanical, or alternate layer while the other deals with consequences in the present or future. Over time, the campaign can expand this into distinct dimensions, linked timelines, and deeper role-specific abilities.

## Core Pillars

### 1. Two-Player Asymmetry

The game is designed for exactly two players.

Each player should feel like they are playing a complete character, not half of a single solution. Their roles should create different responsibilities in combat, exploration, puzzle solving, and dungeon interpretation.

Asymmetry should come from:

- primary role identity
- unique active abilities
- different dungeon layers or dimensions
- different information revealed to each player
- combat tools that solve different tactical problems
- puzzle interactions only one player can perform

The players should need to talk, infer, coordinate, and occasionally trust each other without having identical views of the world.

### 2. Dungeon Crawler First

The game should still feel like a real dungeon crawler:

- rooms, corridors, locked gates, keys, traps, treasure, bosses, secrets
- exploration loops with return paths and shortcuts
- enemy encounters that shape how players read a room
- long-term upgrades and campaign progression
- themed dungeon regions with unique hazards and puzzle languages

Puzzles should enhance the dungeon-crawling identity rather than replace it.

### 3. Combat As A Major Pillar

Combat is not a side activity. Enemies are part of exploration and puzzle solving.

Combat should:

- create pressure during puzzle trials
- teach dungeon mechanics through enemy behavior
- make each role's powers matter moment to moment
- gate some upgrades through combat-puzzle encounters
- reward coordination instead of simple damage races

Some enemies can be puzzle objects as much as combat threats. Examples:

- enemies that must be lured onto sigils
- shielded enemies visible only to one player
- enemies whose deaths change the other player's room state
- future-world enemies created by past-world mistakes
- bosses with different mechanics on each player's screen

### 4. Puzzle Trials With Real Rewards

Puzzles should frequently grant meaningful upgrades, not only open doors.

Trial rewards can include:

- new role abilities
- stronger combat moves
- dimensional traversal tools
- passive stat upgrades
- new ways to read hidden information
- shortcuts or optional dungeon routes

Puzzles can be difficult and should support riddles, deduction, and logic. The best puzzle trials should combine reasoning with combat execution, so solving the idea and surviving the room both matter.

### 5. Layered Worlds And Timelines

The mind-bending hook is that players can occupy different parts of reality.

Possible layers:

- Past and future versions of the same dungeon
- Living world and spirit world
- Physical dungeon and hidden logic/circuit layer
- Dream/curse dimension and waking-world dungeon
- Surface ruins and buried ancient machine

The key design rule: actions in one layer should produce readable, interesting consequences in the other.

Examples:

- Player A burns roots in the past, opening a stone passage for Player B in the future.
- Player B defeats a corrupted knight in the future, revealing the name needed for Player A's riddle in the past.
- Player A rotates ancient machinery, changing trap timing in Player B's dungeon.
- Player B lights future beacons that appear as ghost clues to Player A.

## Primary Role Directions

These are initial role sketches, not locked classes.

### The Warden

The Warden is the physical anchor: durable, direct, tactical, and good at surviving dangerous rooms.

Possible strengths:

- shield, guard, parry, bash, shove
- can hold pressure plates or brace mechanisms
- can redirect enemy attacks into puzzle objects
- can survive hazards long enough to create openings
- can protect or rescue the other player during linked encounters

Puzzle identity:

- manipulates heavy objects
- controls doors, chains, weights, bridges, and ancient locks
- interacts with the dungeon as a material place

Combat identity:

- front-line fighter
- enemy positioning
- counterattacks
- room control

### The Seer

The Seer is the hidden-truth role: fragile or precise, but able to read what the dungeon conceals.

Possible strengths:

- reveals invisible symbols, traps, paths, and enemy weaknesses
- marks enemies or objects for the Warden
- reads riddles, ghost speech, runes, and false walls
- manipulates light, memory, echoes, or time traces
- can briefly phase into another layer of the dungeon

Puzzle identity:

- interprets clues
- changes hidden logic states
- finds correct sequences, names, symbols, and routes
- communicates information the other player cannot see

Combat identity:

- ranged, magical, evasive, debuff-focused
- exposes weak points
- controls cursed enemies
- enables combo attacks

## Future Role Expansion

Primary roles should exist from the start. Secondary roles can come later as campaign systems.

Possible future secondary role sources:

- relics
- dungeon schools
- vows
- masks
- spirit contracts
- weapon lineages
- elemental blessings

These should modify the primary role rather than erase it.

## Campaign Structure

This is a long campaign, not a short session game.

Potential structure:

- Prologue dungeon teaches movement, combat, communication, and role asymmetry.
- Act 1 introduces separate information and basic linked-room puzzles.
- Act 2 introduces stronger time/layer interactions.
- Act 3 introduces full dimensional separation and role-specific dungeon routes.
- Late campaign expects players to combine combat mastery, riddles, and cross-world logic.

Each major dungeon should have:

- a visual theme
- a puzzle language
- enemy families that teach or stress that puzzle language
- at least one role-specific upgrade
- secrets requiring optional cooperation
- a boss encounter that expresses the dungeon's central mechanic

## Visual Direction

The target feel is 16-bit SNES-inspired, but not merely nostalgic.

Useful references in spirit:

- readable tile-based environments
- expressive limited animation
- strong silhouettes
- dramatic color palettes per dungeon layer
- clear combat telegraphs
- UI that feels like an adventure RPG rather than a modern dashboard

The art should support different realities. The same room can have two related palettes or tile treatments so each player's world feels connected but distinct.

Possible tonal blend:

- dark mysterious dungeon
- weird magical machinery
- haunted castle puzzle-box
- occasional brighter adventure-fantasy contrast

## First Prototype Target

The first playable prototype should prove the game loop, not the whole campaign.

Recommended prototype:

- 2 players in one connected dungeon wing
- one Warden-like role
- one Seer-like role
- basic combat with 2-3 enemy types
- one linked puzzle room where actions in one layer affect the other
- one combat trial that rewards an ability upgrade
- one boss or mini-boss requiring asymmetric coordination

Success criteria:

- both players have interesting things to do
- communication is useful but not tedious
- combat and puzzles reinforce each other
- the SNES-like presentation is readable and appealing
- the linked-world mechanic feels like the game's identity

## Open Design Questions

- Are both players always visible in the same shared camera/space, or do they often have separate screens?
- Should the game support local co-op, online co-op, or both?
- Is the perspective top-down Zelda-like, side-view dungeon platformer, or grid/room-based crawler?
- How punishing should death be?
- Should campaign progression be linear, hub-based, or map-based?
- How much text/riddle content should exist compared with environmental logic?
- How often should players split between dimensions versus reunite in the same space?
- Should the Warden/Seer names stay, or are they temporary labels?
